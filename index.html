Atomic Clocks &
GPS Accuracy

How the world's most precise timekeepers make GPS navigation possible
±3 nanoseconds
🛰️
Every time Google Maps pinpoints your location, it relies on atomic clocks aboard GPS satellites. These clocks are so precise they lose less than 1 second every 100 million years. Without them, your GPS location could be off by kilometres — making navigation useless.
Core Concepts
⚛️
What is an Atomic Clock?
A clock that measures time using the vibrations of atoms — typically caesium or rubidium. Atoms vibrate at a perfectly constant frequency, making them the most reliable timekeepers known to science.
📡
How GPS Uses Time
GPS works by measuring how long a signal takes to travel from satellite to your phone. Since signals travel at the speed of light, even a tiny timing error causes a huge location error — 1 nanosecond = 30 cm of error.
🌍
Trilateration
Your GPS receiver calculates its position by measuring signals from at least 4 satellites simultaneously. The intersection of those timed signals pinpoints exactly where you are on Earth.
How It Works — Step by Step
01
Atomic Clock Ticks Aboard Satellite
Each of the 31 GPS satellites carries 3–4 atomic clocks. They tick 9,192,631,770 times per second — the natural vibration frequency of a caesium-133 atom.
02
Satellite Broadcasts Timed Signal
The satellite continuously broadcasts a radio signal stamped with the exact time it was sent, along with the satellite's precise orbital position.
03
Your Device Receives the Signal
Your phone receives signals from multiple satellites. It notes the arrival time of each and calculates how long each signal took to travel.
04
Location is Calculated
Using the travel times and the speed of light, your device calculates its exact distance from each satellite and finds where those spheres of distance overlap — your location.
Why Atomic Clocks Make GPS Better
🎯
Pinpoint Accuracy
Atomic clocks accurate to 3 nanoseconds give location accuracy of under 1 metre. A quartz watch drifting by 1 millisecond would cause a 300 km location error.
🔄
Self-Correcting System
Ground stations constantly compare satellite clocks against master atomic clocks on Earth and send corrections — keeping the entire GPS network synchronised.
⚡
No Signal Degradation
Unlike quartz oscillators that drift over time, atomic clocks maintain the same precision for years — critical for satellites that cannot be serviced in orbit.
🌐
Enables Modern Life
Beyond navigation, atomic clock GPS timing synchronises global internet, stock markets, power grids, and mobile networks — all need nanosecond-level precision.
